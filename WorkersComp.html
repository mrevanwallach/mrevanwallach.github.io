Worker's Compensation

Whether you run a business with hundreds of employess or you're self-employed and you need a work permit, you're probably required by law to maintain workers compensation.

If and when an employee is injured either on the job or as a result of his/her employment, their employer is almost always liable, per state law, to provide compensation to that worker. Workers Compensation claims are governed by the doctrine of "strict liability" - in laymans terms, that means that the employer is responsible whether or not there was any fault or negligence. Claims range from tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars, based on the severity of the injury and amount of time the claimant is out of work.

These expenses include:
  • Medical Coverage: doctors visits, hospital care, prescriptions medicine, and a host of other treatments which may be required based on the specific injury.
  • Disability: partial replacement of an employee's lost income while he or she is unable to work as a result of a work-related injury.
  • Rehabilitation: ranging from physical therapy to vocational training for workers that can't return to their previous occupation due to the nature of their injury.
  • Death Benefits: benefits afforded to the spouse and any minor children of a worker who died from a work related injury.

    • Most employers look at workers' compensation as just another necessary evil and unavoidable cost of doing business. It's usually one of those out of sight, out of mind things when rates are low. It's not until an employer is hit with a claim or a rate hike that they really start to give some thought to their workers' compensation coverage.

    Employers need to look at workers' compensation as a tool to improve their business' bottom line, and make an effort to keep their low rates over the long-term so that they can take advantage of some significant savings.
